National Water and Climate Center, Air & Water Database

The National Water and Climate Center houses data from the SNOTEL network, which is managed by the Natural Resources Conservation Service. Data collected from the SNOTEL network is a particularly important dataset in the western United States becuase these data are used to monitor and forecast water supply derived from mountain snowpack. I chose to work with SNOTEL data, in part, because NCEI data was inaccessible during the week after Hurricane Helene due to severe infrastucture damage. A second reason to explore SNOTEL data is to support a research project that examines how ecosystems are re-establishing on landscapes that were previously inundated by Lake Powell. Over 80,000 acres of land that was once flooded is now a terrestial landscape and is being shaped by erosion and ecological succession. The availability of water and the flow tributaries to Lake Powell play an important role in how the landscape evolves. I chose to examine data from a high elevation SNOTEL site in the Esclante River watershed for this project because I have several research sites within the watershed. The SNOTEL site, Widtsoe #3, is located at 9,640 feet on Boulder Mountain in southern Utah (link to Widtsoe #3 site page). Widtsoe #3 was established in 1978 and is among SNOTEL sites with the longest period of record. Data is collected year-round from Widtsoe #3 and includes information about snow water equivalent (SWE), snow depth, precipitation, temperature and soil moisture.

Maxiumum snow water equivalent and annual precipitation

SNOTEL data from the Widtsoe #3 site was imported as daily data. To understand long-term trends in snow water equivalent (SWE ) and precipitation, the initial dataframe must be manipulated to provide annual maximum SWE accumulation and annual precipitation. Daily SWE and precipitation were recorded as SWE and precipitation acummulation since the start of the water year (October 1). To extract annual maximum SWE and annual precipitation, I used the .resample function by resampling the maximum SWE and precipitation value from each year.

OLS Regression analysis of peak SWE and annual precipitation

Annual maximum SWE data from Widtsoe #3 is somewhat left-skewed, but otherwise looks like a mostly normal distribution. Given the skewedness of the data, it would probably be best to perform a data transformation. Annual precipitation data also has a slight left-skew, but is pretty close to a normal distribution. For the purpose of this exercise, the assumptions needed for an OLS regression are close enough to being met.

Long-term trends in peak SWE and annual precipitation at Widtsoe #3 SNOTEL

Long-term trends are difficult to assess in SNOTEL data due to annual variability in precipitation and the relatively short length of record. Widtsoe #3 was among the first SNOTEL sites installed in the western United States and has a relatively long 46 year record of SWE and precipitation. An analysis of annual maximum or peak SWE using an OLS regression showed a not signficiant trend towards decreasing annual peak SWE (F=0.182, r2=0.084). Annual peak SWE showed a not significant decreasing trend of 0.8" inches/decade. Most likely causes of decreasing annual peak SWE are decreased precipitation or increased temperature leading to more precipitation falling as rain. The analysis of annual precipitation using an OLS regression showed no signficiant trend in precipitation over time (F=0.810, r2=0.001). A regression analysis was not performed with temperature data from Widtsoe #3 because only 20 years of temperature data was available. Some SNOTEL sites in the western United States do show significant decreasing trends in annual peak SWE, but these are typically sites at lower elevations. Snow at higher elevation sites is typically more resilient to warming temperatures because historical winter temperatures were much below freezing. If the trend in decreasing SWE at Widtsoe #3 continues in the future, the most likely cause would be increasing winter temperatures leading to a greater proportion of precipitation falling as rain.
